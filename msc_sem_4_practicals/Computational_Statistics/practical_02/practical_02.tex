\hspace{1cm} Use Monte Carlo Integration to approximate the following integrals
\begin{tasks}(2)

and check how the estimates converge to the true value as the sample size increases.

\section*{\faArrowAltCircleRight[regular] \textcolor{blue}{Theory}}

Suppose we have to compute

\begin{equation}
I = \int \limits_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) dx \hspace{0.2cm} \text{or} \hspace{0.2cm} I = \int \limits_{a}^{\infty} f(x) dx
\end{equation}

We rewrite

\begin{equation}
I = \int f(x) dx = \int \dfrac{f(x)}{p(x)} p(x) dx = E_X \left[ \dfrac{f(x)}{p(x)} \right]
\end{equation}

where $p(x)$ is a \textbf{suitable} probability density and $X \sim p$. \\[0.2em]

To approximate $I$, we generate random numbers $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ from $p(x)$ and calculate

\begin{align}
I \approx \dfrac{1}{n} \sum \limits_{i=1}^{n} \dfrac{f(X_i)}{p(X_i)}.
\end{align}

By law of large numbers, $\dfrac{1}{n} \sum \limits_{i=1}^{n} \dfrac{f(X_i)}{p(X_i)} \rightarrow E_X \left[ \dfrac{f(x)}{p(x)} \right]$ as $n \rightarrow \infty$.
