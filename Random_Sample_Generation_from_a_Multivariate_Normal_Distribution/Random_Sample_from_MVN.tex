While there exists function to generate random sample from a multivariate normal distribution, here we discuss a very simple technique that accomplishes the job. Suppose we have to generate a sample of size $n$ from $N_p\left( \utilde{\mu}, \Sigma \right)$. The steps are as follows :

\begin{enumerate}[Step I.]
\item Generate a sample of size $n \times p$ from $N(0, 1)$. Club each $p$ of them to get a sample of size $n$ from $N_p\left( \utilde{0}, I_{p}\right)$.

\item Consider eigenvalue decomposition of $\Sigma$ \textit{i.e.} $\Sigma = P\Lambda P'$ where $P$ is a $p \times p$ matrix with columns as eigenvectors of $\Sigma$ and $\Lambda$ is a diagonal matrix of order $p$ with eigenvalues of $\Sigma$ in its diagonal entries. $\Sigma$ is a symmetric matrix, so $P$ is an orthogonal matrix. Now,
\begin{align*}
\utilde{X} &\sim N_p\left( \utilde{0}, I_{p} \right) \\[0.2em]
\Rightarrow P \sqrt{\Lambda} \cdot \utilde{X} &\sim N_p\left( \utilde{0}, P \sqrt{\Lambda} \cdot I_{p} \cdot \left(P \sqrt{\Lambda} \right)' \right) \equiv N_p\left( \utilde{0}, P \Lambda P'\right) \\[0.2em]
\Rightarrow \utilde{\mu} + P \sqrt{\Lambda} \cdot \utilde{X} &\sim N_p\left( \utilde{\mu}, \Sigma \right).
\end{align*}

\end{enumerate}

Thus, $\utilde{\mu} + P \sqrt{\Lambda} \cdot \utilde{X}$ becomes a sample from $N_p\left( \utilde{\mu}, \Sigma \right)$.

Suppose we want a random sample of size $10000$ from $N_3 \left( \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \right)$.



\begin{knitrout}
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}
\begin{alltt}
\hldef{n} \hlkwb{<-} \hlnum{10}\hlopt{^}\hlnum{5}\hldef{; p} \hlkwb{<-} \hlnum{3}
\end{alltt}
\end{kframe}
\end{knitrout}

\begin{knitrout}
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}
\begin{alltt}
\hldef{mu} \hlkwb{<-} \hlkwd{matrix}\hldef{(}\hlkwd{c}\hldef{(}\hlnum{2}\hldef{,} \hlnum{1}\hldef{,} \hlnum{2}\hldef{),} \hlkwc{nrow} \hldef{= p)}
\end{alltt}
\end{kframe}
\end{knitrout}

\begin{knitrout}
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}
\begin{alltt}
\hldef{Sigma} \hlkwb{<-} \hlkwd{matrix}\hldef{(}\hlkwd{c}\hldef{(}\hlnum{2}\hldef{,} \hlnum{1}\hldef{,} \hlnum{1}\hldef{,}
                  \hlnum{1}\hldef{,} \hlnum{3}\hldef{,} \hlnum{0}\hldef{,}
                  \hlnum{1}\hldef{,} \hlnum{0}\hldef{,} \hlnum{1}\hldef{),} \hlkwc{nrow} \hldef{= p,} \hlkwc{ncol} \hldef{= p,} \hlkwc{byrow} \hldef{=} \hlnum{TRUE}\hldef{)}
\end{alltt}
\end{kframe}
\end{knitrout}

\begin{knitrout}
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}
\begin{alltt}
\hldef{a} \hlkwb{<-} \hlkwd{matrix}\hldef{(}\hlkwd{rnorm}\hldef{(n} \hlopt{*} \hldef{p,} \hlkwc{mean} \hldef{=} \hlnum{0}\hldef{,} \hlkwc{sd} \hldef{=} \hlnum{1}\hldef{),} \hlkwc{nrow} \hldef{= p,} \hlkwc{ncol} \hldef{= n,} \hlkwc{byrow} \hldef{=} \hlnum{FALSE}\hldef{)}
\end{alltt}
\end{kframe}
\end{knitrout}

Now each column of matrix \textit{a} is a random sample from $N_3 (\utilde{0}, I_3)$.

\begin{knitrout}
\definecolor{shadecolor}{rgb}{0.969, 0.969, 0.969}\color{fgcolor}\begin{kframe}

Matrix \textit{X} should be a matrix with columns as random samples from $N_3 \left( \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \right)$. \\[0.5em]

Let us calculate the sample mean vector and sample variance-covariance matrix.
